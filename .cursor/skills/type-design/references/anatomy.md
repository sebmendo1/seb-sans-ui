# Letterform Anatomy

Full part-names for letterforms, organized so you can look up either "what
is this part called" or "which letters have this part." Use this before
describing or critiquing a specific glyph's shape — precise anatomy
vocabulary is what makes design feedback reviewable by other type-literate
people.

## Serif-adjacent parts (relevant mainly to serif typefaces, but the vocabulary carries over)

- **Serif** — the foot-like stroke finishing a stem's end.
  - **Bracketed** — curved support joining the serif to the main stroke.
  - **Unbracketed** — no curved support, sharper transition.
  - **Wedge** — serif thinner at its end than its start.
  - **Slab** — uniform, thick serif.
  - **Hairline** — uniform, very thin serif.
  - **Cupped** — the horizontal segment curves inward to counteract the
    illusion that it's bending (seen in EB Garamond's large serifs).
- **Beak** — a small vertical serif appearing on just the top of a curved
  letter (G, S).
- **Spur** — a small serif at the join between two strokes. *This is the
  term for Seb Sans's frozen "spurred G" trait* — GT America's signature
  American-gothic marker.
- **Flag** — a horizontal serif to one side of a vertical stem, classically
  seen on numeral 1 (Seb Sans's frozen "footed 1" is functionally a flag).

## Terminal types (the end of a stroke that isn't a serif)

- **Terminal** — general term for a stroke's un-serifed end.
  - **Teardrop** — rounded, bulb-like terminal.
  - **Ball** — circular terminal.
  - **Flare** — terminal that widens gradually.
  - **Shear** — terminal cut at an angle.
  - **Calligraphic** — terminal shaped as if cut by a broad pen.
- **Finial** — a curved or tapered terminal specific to open letters like
  c and e.
- **Tail** — a stroke that finishes below the baseline (as in Q, or
  lowercase y/j descenders).
  - **Swash** — a flourish substituted for a plain tail.
- **Hook** — the curved top of a lowercase f.

## Enclosed and semi-enclosed spaces

- **Counter** — the fully or partially enclosed negative space inside a
  letter (o, e, n, the bowl of b/p/d/q).
  - **Eye** — the specific term for the counter inside a lowercase e.
  - **Loop** — the specific term for the counter in a double-story
    lowercase g's lower story.
- **Link** — the stroke joining a double-story g's upper story (the "eye"
  part) to its loop below.
- **Ear** — a small stroke finishing off a link or bowl, often on g or r.

## Strokes and joins

- **Stem** — the main vertical or diagonal stroke (both verticals of an H).
- **Shoulder** — the curved join between a vertical stem and a stroke
  reaching off it, as in n, m, h — where the arch begins.
- **Leg** — the diagonal stem in k or R.
- **Arm** — a horizontal stroke attached at only one end, as in the arms
  of an E.
- **Crossbar** — a horizontal stroke joining two vertical stems (the bar
  of an H or A).
- **Waist** — the cinched center point of a double-story letter like B, R,
  or K.
- **Spine** — the curved, S-shaped vertical stroke central to s (and the
  numeral 8).
- **Throat** — the short vertical stem just before G's opening.
- **Vertex** — the point where two diagonal strokes meet near the *bottom*
  (as in V).
- **Apex** — the point where two diagonal strokes meet near the *top* (as
  in A).

## Dots and marks

- **Dot / tittle** — the mark above a lowercase i or j. *This is the term
  formalized by Seb Sans's "the Point" DNA trait* — enlarged 6% and held
  perfectly circular through every width transformation.

## Bowls and rounded forms

- **Bowl** — the curved stroke enclosing a rounded letter's counter (O, b,
  p, the main curve of a lowercase a's lower story).
  - Optical rule: the thickest point of a bowl's curve should exceed the
    typeface's vertical stem width; the thinnest point should be less than
    the horizontal stem width, or the bowl looks structurally
    inconsistent with the straight strokes around it.

## Optical-adjustment vocabulary (not physical parts, but essential terms)

- **Overshoot** — round/pointed letters extending slightly past an
  alignment line (baseline, cap height, x-height) to read as optically the
  same size as flat letters that sit exactly on the line.
- **Relief** — the small visible notch created where a bowl meets a
  vertical stem (in letters like p, d, b, and more prominently in n's
  shoulder-to-stem join, and even more in r's — r's side is open, so its
  notch needs to sit lower than n/m/h's to compensate).
- **Optical center** — the visually-correct (as opposed to mathematically
  correct) position for a double-story letter's crossbar/waist; always
  slightly above true mathematical center, because true center reads as
  too low.

## Grouping letters by shared shape (useful for propagating a design decision)

**Uppercase:**
- Round: O Q C G S
- Square: E F H I L T
- Diagonal: V A W X
- Round-square hybrid: D B P R
- Diagonal-square hybrid: M N K Z Y

**Lowercase (bowl-based family, all inherit from o):**
- o, c, e, p, d, b, q, g — c and e need narrower bowls than o since their
  open counters already read as visually large.

**Lowercase (stem+shoulder family, all inherit from n):**
- n, m, h, u, r — r needs an even lower relief/notch than the others
  because its right side is fully open, not closed by a second stem.

**The outlier:** lowercase **a** — double-story, open counter on the left,
borrows its arch proportions from n's shoulder but doesn't taper
identically. Typically the hardest control letter to get right; bowl
height usually 55–65% of x-height.
